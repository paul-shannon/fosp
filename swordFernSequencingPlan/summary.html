• Younginger 2017 and Brader 2017 (see below) predict that the Polystichum munitum microbiome is a dynamic seasonally varying mix of interacting fungi, bacteria and oomycetes, some of which are protective, some pathogenic, some neutral.

  • A perturbation to the microbiome could be the cause of the Puget Lowland sword fern die-off. Endophyte sequencing may illuminate this.

  • The hypothesized perturbation may be as simple as the gain of a single pathogen or the loss of a single protective species.

  • Equally possible is that the perturbation is a shift in the dynamics of complex interacting populations of fungi, bacteria and oomycetes, perhaps with environmental abiotic drivers.

  • We need site selection, sampling and analysis procedures which accomodate both possibilities: simple single species causality as well complex interactive dynamics.

  • Therefore we propose a pilot study to
    1. reproduce Younginger's methods and findings in healthy ferns
    2. then apply those same methods to affected ferns

  • Note that Younginger found high heterogeneitity of fungal OTUs in April which increased through May, then declined to a small number by August, with one OTU eventually dominating. These trends repeated across the seasons at four sites remote from each other.

  • We will need some scaled down, Seward-appropriate, and affordable simplification of Younginger's sample collection protocol. In particular, Younginger's sampling through the year seems to be requried. He selected
    ...four fronds on 20 plants along two transects, each 100 m long and separated by 50 m. No two sample plants were touching and at least 3 m apart. Sampling was conducted approximately every 30 days for 10 months total.
