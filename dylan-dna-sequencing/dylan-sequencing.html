oct 4. Dylan Reports Extraction Problems
I've encountered some issues with the quality of the final extracts so it looks like I will need to spend some time optimizing the DNA extraction workflow. I was hoping it would be a more streamlined process of following the protocol, but troubleshooting things like this is not uncommon in molecular biology.
Background
I've been working with a small number of test samples from extra sword fern pinnae that I collected to make sure that we're getting good results before using the kit on the actual samples. I measured the spectra of the DNA extracts and calculated the 260/280 and 260/230 purity ratios. The values that I got were below the acceptable range which indicates potential contamination. The concern is that these contaminants could be PCR inhibitors.
Findings
The 260/280 ratios for DNA should ideally be above 1.8. I had values range from 1.60 to 1.86, which indicates potential contamination from proteins or phenols. Proteins would be inherited from the tissue samples. Phenols could be from the plant tissue and/or from reagents that carried over from earlier steps in the workflow.

The 260/230 ratios should ideally be around 2.0, but I had values as low as 0.56 and 1.06. This is likely caused by contamination from salts, proteins or cellulose.

Total DNA yield was adequate: 30 to 50 ng/ul. It should be 5 ng/ul at a minimum.

I think the poor quality is most likely the result of me overloading the reagents with more plant tissue than you're supposed to use. I was trying to carefully follow Younginger's protocol, and his methods clearly indicate that he extracted DNA from the entire pinna. But that meant that I was adding 3 to 12 times more tissue than is specified in the current Qiagen kit protocol (50 to 100 mg). This may be a slightly different protocol than what Younginger used, which has since been discontinued / updated.

Another contributing factor is probably the unique biochemistry of sword fern leaves - old perennial fronds that are probably rich in phenols and complex organic compounds. DNA extraction from younger or softer plant tissue is generally thought to be easier.

Next Steps
I'd like to do some further troubleshooting to optimize the protocol. This will involve varying the amount of tissue used, and potentially augmenting other steps in the protocol. For example, the kit comes with a special solution that is completely optional - it reduces overall yield but improves quality. If protocol optimization hasn't brought the purity ratios back up to acceptable levels, I can also try some diagnostic PCR. If I'm able to create PCR products using the same primers as Dalhousie, it doesn't matter much what the 260/280 or 260/230 values are. If it works, it works.

In terms of timeline, this probably looks like doing optimization work in October and extracting DNA / submitting samples for sequencing in early to mid November.

Please let me know what you think of this plan or if you have any questions.

oct 28: Paul Talbert Discusses Protocols
Hi Dylan:

Sorry to be slow to respond. I'm not sure where you are in your trouble-shooting, but I see a number of differences in the DNeasy Plant Mini Kit that Younginger used and the DNeasy Plant Pro kit you used, mostly having to do with somewhat different buffers. Those seem unlikely to be the source of purity problems to me. I think you are probably correct that overloading the tissue is the likely reason for impurities. The other step I would look at carefully is the tissue homogenization and disruption.

You say that the Younginger "methods clearly indicate that he extracted DNA from the entire pinna." but I didn't find this to be entirely clear. He says that he surface-sterilized pinnae and placed them in tubes for disruption of "fresh tissue" in a Tissuelyzer II bead mill, "immediately preceding DNA extraction... according to the manufacturer's instructions." Both the DNeasy Plant Mini and DNeasy Plant Pro kits specify 100mg or less of wet tissue, so he may have used less than an entire pinna. Regardless of whether he extracted DNA from the entire pinna or from just 100 mg, I think I would try testing DNA prepared from <100mg of tissue as recommended to see if that solves your problem. It's possible that the Pro Kit may be more sensitive to the quantity of material than the Mini Kit.

The Pro kit offers 3 methods of homogenization and I would guess that this is a critical step for clean preps. The mini Kit recommends a Tissuelyzer II, Tissue Ruptor, or a mortar and pestle, but Younginger says he used the Tissuelyzer II. I haven't used these kits so I am not sure how this step works, and you don't mention which method you used. The Tissuelyzer II and Powerlyzer 24 Homogenizer appear to be a separate pieces of (probably expensive) equipment. Do you have access to one of these, or did you use the vortex method? Grinding tissue with a mortar and pestle is used in a lot of different DNA preps, but you'd need liquid nitrogen and this is usually used for larger quantities (grams, not milligrams) of tissue than just an individual pinna, so I'm not sure that would be a good choice even if it were recommended in the Pro kit.

You also mention that you could do a PCR test to see if you get products (of the expected size). If you have the primers, this seems like a quick one-day way of seeing if your samples are sufficiently pure.

Calling Qiagen customer service wouldn't hurt either. Sometimes they have no better idea than you do , but other times they may know how to help, or at least be a sounding board for your ideas of how to trouble shoot. They probably have the best information about differences between the two kits, homogenization methods, and tissue quantities.

Let me know if I can be of help.

Dec 8: Dylan's monthly report
Yes, primers arrived last week. The lab manager and I also inspected the nanodrop to verify that it is providing accurate readings. It had 260/230 values in the expected range using an independent DNA standard, so that suggests that it is definitely our challenging sword fern samples, and not the machine.

I didn't hear back from Brett Younginger, but I'm not too surprised given how busy he is these days. I did talk to Qiagen customer service though. They had several encouraging suggestions:

  • Repeat the two wash steps (ie run each of them 2x)
  • Do a dry spin after the final wash to make sure all ethanol is gone prior to elution
  • Alternatively, move forward with PCR / amplicon sequencing without changing the protocol
The person that I talked to said that the low 260/230 values might not be very meaningful, especially at relatively low yields such as this. She also thought it was less likely to be polyphenol contamination, and more likely to be residual ethanol. In any case, she suggested trying out PCR and if it works, it works.

I've also reached out to the sequencing vendor to check what their quality control requirements are. Even if we can show that we can do PCR, they still might not accept the DNA extracts (I wouldn't if I was them). It's possible they will require us to send purified PCR products, in which case I will need to order a PCR cleanup kit. These are fairly expensive, so I would like to first see if the suggestions from Qiagen improve the 260/280 and 260/230 values.
