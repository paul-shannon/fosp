The Seward Park forest is shaped by fire and rain.

By rain: the trees - douglas-fir in particular - grow tall and thick. We get lots of rain, but huge storms are rare. So tall trees, though they may (as we will see) lose some of their top, are still able to grow tall.

But not much of our rain falls in the summer. But summer is when sunlight is most abunduant, so these trees need big water storage capacity for peak summertime growth.

So they, like camels, imbibe and store water (in the wet winter) for when they need it later (for sun-fueled growth in the summer).

By fire. The doug-fir is also finely adapted to fire. Here on the wet side of the Cascades, severe fires are infrequent, happening on average every 500 to 1000 years. More often when weather patterns are in flux, as they were in the Little Ice Age, 1300 - 1750.

A massive and severe lowland fire swept the region in 1508, burning perhaps 10 million acres, from the Olympic to the Cascades. Our great tree was born after that fire, looking just like the photo above.
