Sword Fern Decline in the Puget Lowland

Introduction
We first noticed dramatic die-off of Western Sword Fern (Polystichum munitum) in 2013 in the urban old-growth forest in Seattle's Seward Park. In the years since, the die-off has spread - albeit incompletely - throughout the 120 acres of this forest. No co-occuring species appear to be affected. We have tracked the spread, run experiments, consulted plant pathologists and ecologists, reaching the hypothesize that a species-specific pathogen, or perhaps a complex perturbation of the fern's microbiome, are responsible for the die-off. Climate change may be indirectly involved.

In the summer of 2024, as we report below, we established that sword fern mortality is now present and spreading in some sites across the 7000 square miles of the Puget Lowlands.

No comprehensive study of sword fern life history has yet been published. Background sword fern mortality rates are unknown. But we do know that sword ferns typically colonize disturbed ground under an open sky, such as after fire or flood, and that once established, they rarely reproduce and are very long lived. I very much like the idea that they are old individuals, established at a remote time when there was disturbance in the forest. Fire would create such a disturbance. As to age, 1000 years is not out of the question, possibly much longer but there are no data at all for deciding. There are very few sites for the establishment of new plants via gametophyte in the stable mature forests of the Northwest, though a single treefall would create such an opportunity. Polystichum munitum is essentially unbranched, which lends support to the idea that the populations are made up of independently originated individuals, not fragments of some giant clone.
This sketch of sword fern life history, and the findings we present below, suggest that the mortality we report here is highly unusual, possibly related to anthropogenic factors, and is driven primarily by a pathogen or a perturbed microbiome. The loss of this foundational PNW Douglas-Fir species, in combination with the very slow natural regeneration of bare ground beneath a closed forest canopy, means that sword fern mortality is an ongoing threat to overall forest health. It deserves close study.

In this report we summarize twelve years of observations and experiments carried out by citizen scientists, academic researchers, and undergraduate students from the University of Washington. Though we have not determined the cause of mortality, our preliminary data - observations, experiments, and geographic surveys - set the stage for its discovery. As with the recent resolution of seastar wasting disease, we anticipate that detailed laboratory work and DNA sequencing will be needed, some instances of which now (Autumn 2025) have just begun.
