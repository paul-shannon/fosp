Sword Fern Decline in the Puget Lowland

Introduction
We first noticed a dramatic die-off of Western Sword Fern (Polystichum munitum) in 2013 in the urban old-growth forest in Seattle's Seward Park. A pure stand, one quarter acre in size, quickly passed from healthy to dead. In the years since, the die-off has spread - albeit incompletely - throughout the 120 acres of this forest. No co-occuring species appear to be affected.

In response, we have tracked the spread, run experiments, consulted plant pathologists and forest ecologists. Our original hunch has been strengthened, but not yet proven: that a sword-fern-specific pathogen, or perhaps a complex perturbation of the fern's microbiome, are responsible for the die-off. Climate change may be indirectly involved.

In the summer of 2024, as we report below, we established that sword fern mortality is now present and spreading in some sites across the 7000 square miles of the Puget Lowlands.

But is it novel? Is it consequential? If all sword ferns at a site were linked by clonality, it is (barely) conceivable that they could all die in the same year. But clonality is uncommon. And we see mortality progress geographically, moving outward from an affected site.

No comprehensive study of sword fern life history has yet been published. Background sword fern mortality rates are unknown. But we do know that sword ferns typically colonize disturbed ground under an open sky, such as after fire or flood, and that once established, rarely reproduce and are very long lived. Polystichum genus systematist David Barrington, in a personal communications, offers that

I very much like the idea that they are old individuals, established at a remote time when there was disturbance in the forest. Fire would create such a disturbance. As to age, 1000 years is not out of the question, possibly much longer but there are no data at all for deciding. There are very few sites for the establishment of new plants via gametophyte in the stable mature forests of the Northwest, though a single treefall would create such an opportunity. Polystichum munitum is essentially unbranched, which lends support to the idea that the populations are made up of independently originated individuals, not fragments of some giant clone.
This sketch of sword fern life history, and the findings we present below, suggest that the mortality we report here is highly unusual, possibly related to anthropogenic factors, and is driven primarily by a pathogen or a perturbed microbiome. The loss of this foundational PNW Douglas-Fir species, in combination with the very slow natural regeneration of bare ground beneath a closed forest canopy, means that sword fern mortality poses a threat to overall forest health. It deserves close study.

In this report we summarize twelve years of observations and experiments carried out by citizen scientists, academic researchers, and undergraduate students from the University of Washington. Though we have not determined the cause of mortality, our preliminary data - observations, experiments, and geographic surveys - set the stage for its discovery. As with the recent resolution of seastar wasting disease, we anticipate that detailed laboratory work and DNA sequencing will be needed, some instances of which now (Autumn 2025) have just begun.

We present the most important of them here, making the case that the die-off is

  1. Novel

2015 WSU Plant Pathologist Marianne Elliott Finds No Evidence of Phytophthora Infection Dr. Elliott has researched and published widely on phytophthoras in Washington state. In an early visit to Seward Park, she proposed, and for two years we collaborated, testing the hypothesis that a Phytophthora species is responsible for the die-off.

More than 10,000 bare root cedars were planted across Seward's forest in 2011. Only a handful survived. The deed saplings were very much in evidence at Ground Zero. However, using rhododendron bait and a standard protocol, sword fern samples from Seward kept in culture produced no result.

By April 2017, Marianne concluded that other causes should be considered.

2018: The 36 fern (3x12) nursery fern 4-year experiment With some guidance from Verdant LLC, and the support of Seattle Parks, Suzanne Bouchard and I planted young nursery sword ferns at and near Ground Zero in Seward Park. This is an ongoing experiment, to last at least three more years. You can read an earlier report here. The die-off at Ground Zero had reached steady state by 2014, leaving almost entirely bare ground. 10% of the original ferns, as we assessed in a June 2019 study. This quarter acre was subject to erosion and likely undergoing further ecological degradation: underground mycorrhizal networks need photosynthesizing plants, of which there were very few. No natural restoration had taken place until a few fringecups - most happily - appeared in 2019.

Suzanne and planted three lines of 12 ferns, two lines at Ground Zero, 1 line just north of what was then the boundary of the expanding die-off region. I hypothesized that the agent of the die-off, whatever it turns out to be, was no longer active at Ground Zero, but that it was likely to be active and virulent at the die-off's leading edge.

This hypothesis was a generalization from a single fern planted at Ground Zero in 2014. Inspecting the die-off with Seattle Parks plant ecologist Jillian Weed, I asked, "What should we do?". "Monitor closely", she said, "and why don't you plant a couple of nursery ferns?". We did, I watered the pair through a couple of summers, and one (dubbed "Jillian Weed #1") is thriving now six years later. A 50% survival rate for restoration planting is better than I usually achieve. So I surmised that the die-off agent/s were gone - at least temporarily.

I watered all 36 plants weekly during summer drought, two liters per plant, for three summers. The Ground Zero ferns (with one exception) are thriving. The northern line ("ADZ" for active die-off zone) is about 50% dead or dying. I have made every attempt to treat all three lines identically. By 2024, the one questionable ADZ fern has recovered, and is now in good health.

In April, Bonnie Drew, Jeff Kelley and I twice independently surveyed all three lines. We counted fiddleheads, interpreting them as simple markers of overall plant health. I provisionally conclude that there is currently no active agent affecting ferns at Ground Zero. The degraded site is now recovering, which is very fine to see. The agent may return.

2018: The Beer Bottle Experiment An informal experiment suggested that the agent/s of the die-off can be reproducibly transmitted from an affected frond to a healthy one via water. Healthy and affected frond pairs were placed in bottles filled with tap water, with some healthy/healthy pairings used as controls. After three weeks, the healthy frond from the healthy/affected pairing showed signs of necrosis. The control healthy/healthy pairings were largely unchanged. This contrast lends some support for water borne pathogen transmission of the presumed disease.
