Sword Fern Decline in the Puget Lowland

Introduction #1

A dramatic die-off of the long-lived, robust, drought-tolerant, dominant understory sword fern of PNW lowland douglas-fir forests was first observed in 2013 in an old-growth urban forest in Seattle's Seward Park.

It has since been observed throughout the Puget Lowlands.

Sword ferns need bare mineralized soil and open sunlight to take root and grow, so within mature forests with intact canopies, sword fern regeneration is rare.

Natural regeneration of other species is also very slow after the ferns die. Twelve years have passed at the original Ground Zero die-off site, with nearly zero natural regeneration. We worry that long-term bare ground raises the dangers of cascading ecosystem decline in this already stressed, rare and beautiful old-growth urban forests.

The Friends of Seward Park (FoSP), Seattle Parks, and several academic researchers have studied, and puzzled over this now regional problem for more then ten years. The specific cause (or causes) remain unknown, but overlapping evidence strongly suggests a biotic cause, possibly introduced, possibly endemic, possibly amplified by changes in weather patterns.

In this report we present that evidence and describe currently ongoing projects.

Introduction # 2

We first noticed a dramatic die-off of Western Sword Fern (Polystichum munitum) in 2013 in the urban old-growth forest in Seattle's Seward Park. A pure stand, one quarter acre in size, quickly passed from healthy to dead. In the years since, the die-off has spread - albeit incompletely - throughout the 120 acres of this forest. No co-occuring species appear to be affected.

In response, we have tracked the spread, run experiments, consulted plant pathologists and forest ecologists. Our original hunch has been strengthened, but not yet proven: that a sword-fern-specific pathogen, or perhaps a complex perturbation of the fern's microbiome, are responsible for the die-off. Climate change may be indirectly involved.

In the summer of 2024, as we report below, we established that sword fern mortality is now present and spreading in some sites across the 7000 square miles of the Puget Lowlands.

But is it novel? Is it consequential? If all sword ferns at a site were linked by clonality, it is (barely) conceivable that they could all die in the same year. But clonality is uncommon. And we see mortality progress geographically, moving outward from an affected site.

No comprehensive study of sword fern life history has yet been published. Background sword fern mortality rates are unknown. But we do know that sword ferns typically colonize disturbed ground under an open sky, such as after fire or flood, and that once established, rarely reproduce and are very long lived. Polystichum genus systematist David Barrington, in a personal communications, offers that

I very much like the idea that they are old individuals, established at a remote time when there was disturbance in the forest. Fire would create such a disturbance. As to age, 1000 years is not out of the question, possibly much longer but there are no data at all for deciding. There are very few sites for the establishment of new plants via gametophyte in the stable mature forests of the Northwest, though a single treefall would create such an opportunity. Polystichum munitum is essentially unbranched, which lends support to the idea that the populations are made up of independently originated individuals, not fragments of some giant clone.
This sketch of sword fern life history, and the findings we present below, suggest that the mortality we report here is highly unusual, possibly related to anthropogenic factors, and is driven primarily by a pathogen or a perturbed microbiome. The loss of this foundational PNW Douglas-Fir species, in combination with the very slow natural regeneration of bare ground beneath a closed forest canopy, means that sword fern mortality poses a threat to overall forest health. It deserves close study.

In this report we summarize twelve years of observations and experiments carried out by citizen scientists, academic researchers, and undergraduate students from the University of Washington. Though we have not determined the cause of mortality, our preliminary data - observations, experiments, and geographic surveys - set the stage for its discovery. As with the recent resolution of seastar wasting disease, we anticipate that detailed laboratory work and DNA sequencing will be needed, some instances of which now (Autumn 2025) have just begun.

Observations and Experiments 2013-2025 The full history of observations and experiments are documented here, with the most important presented below. These make the case that the die-off is
  1. Regionally and historically novel
  2. Caused by unknown biotic factors
  3. Once introduced at a stie, it progresses at variable rates
  4. Occurs intermittently across the Puget Lowlands
  5. Deserves continued close study until its etiology is understood, responses and possible remedies identified.
2015 WSU Plant Pathologist Marianne Elliott Finds No Evidence of Phytophthora Infection Dr. Elliott has published widely on phytophthoras in Washington state, including research into the introduction of pathogens from regional PNW native plant nurseries. In 2015 visit to "Ground Zero" at Seward Park - a quarter acre site in which 90% of the pure stand of sword ferns had died - Marianne suggested that a Phytophthora species may be responsible. More than 10,000 bare root cedars were planted across Seward's forest in 2011, of which only a handful survived. The dead saplings were very much in evidence at Ground Zero.

Over the next two years, using samples delivered to her lab, and working pro bono, Marianne cconcluded that phytophthoras seemed unlikely, and that other causes should be considered.

2018: The 36 fern (3x12) nursery fern 4-year experiment We hypothesize two contrasting modes by which infection of the presumed pathogen travels: contiguous and "leaping" (non-contiguous) spread. In this experiment, that ran for four years, we emphasize contiguous spread. (Our support for leaping spread is anecdotal: we regularly see fern mortality emerge in distant parts of the forest, with no obvious animal vector, water flow or nearby pre-existing infection.) In January of 2018, supported by Seattle Parks, FoSP planted 36 young nursery sword ferns at and near Ground Zero in Seward Park. The ferns were divided into three lines of 12 ferns each, two lines planted at Ground Zero, and one line planted just north of what was then the boundary of the expanding die-off region. We hypothesized that the agent of the die-off, whatever it may be, was no longer active at Ground Zero, but was likely to be active and virulent at the die-off's leading edge.

All 36 ferns were planted with the same method. We watered all 36 plants weekly during summer drought, two liters per plant, for three summers. Now seven years later, all of the Ground Zero ferns are thriving, where the northern line ("ADZ" for active die-off zone) is 70% dead. The first mortality in that line appeared in the first year.

New frond (fiddlehead) production is a reasonable proxy of plant health, so each year for four years, in spring, we counted new fronds. summarized here in box plot form. The full dataset is available on request.

We provisionally conclude that the active agent which killed Ground Zero fronds in 2013 is no longer present at Ground Zero. This suggests that the agent did its work, traveled on, and has no direct path by which to reinfect these new Ground Zero ferns.

The agent may yet return, as an instance of leaping spread, or via contiguous spread if restoration efforts re-establish a path from an ongoing die-off site. But after seven years, the twelve Ground Zero nursery ferns are all healthy.

These data confirmed out prediction. After four years, the contiguous die-off spread north. Several of the 12 ADZ ferns began to fail within the first year after planting. By 2025, 7 of the 12 are dead, and 2 are in poor condition. All 24 GZ ferns are in good health, producing new fronds each year.

2018: The Beer Bottle Experiment We conducted an informal experiment looking for evidence of waterborne transmission of a possible pathogen. We cut twelve complete fronds from the forest, nine of them from healthy plants, threefrom dying plants. Six casually cleaned beer bottles were filled with tap water. Three bottles then received healthy/healthy frond pairs. Three bottles recieved healthy/affected ffront pairs. We watched the ferns for three weeks, photographing them to keep a record. To our surprise, we got a result. We found that, after three weeks, in the healthy/affected bottles the healthy fronds looked like the diseased fronds: wilted, starting to turn brown. Meanwhile in the healthy/healthy pairings, the fronds had wilted a little bit - as you might expect after three weeks in a beer bottle - but they were distinctly different from the affected/healthy pairings. This suggests that there may be a waterborne pathogen which had moved within the bottle, from the affected frond to the healthy one.
November 2019: A more rigorous transmission experiment
With funds donated from Seattle's 100 Women Who Care, FoSP hired Dylan Mendenhall to repeat the beer bottle experiment, testing for possible pathogen transmission with laboratory protocols. His conclusion:
The regional decline of sword ferns (Polystichum munitum) in the Puget Sound lowlands remains unexplained. However, anecdotal observations and some preliminary evidence from a pilot study suggest that a transmissible pathogen may be driving the phenomenon. We used sword fern fronds as a bioassay in a paired-sample greenhouse experiment to determine if symptoms could be transmitted from symptomatic fronds to healthy fronds with water as a shared medium. After 5 days, fronds in the infected treatment group were significantly more desiccated and had significantly lower foliar moisture content than fronds in the control group which were grown in water with apparently healthy fronds. We were not able to identify whether the causal agent was transmitted through physical contact, an aboveground vector or through the water. Nonetheless, our results suggest that a vascular pathogen is present in the foliage of sword ferns located at the edge of an active die-off site in Seward Park.
Summer 2025: Regional Survey of the Puget Lowlands
We started an iNaturalist project in 2018: Western Sword Fern Decline in the Pacific Northwest Nearly 200 reports have been entered there, most of them credible, most of them displaying symptoms similar to what we see at Seward Park.

These reports help to establish the presence of the sword fern blight beyond Seattle's Seward Park. However, the reproducability and reliability of the reports is in question. These reports were not made by trained observers following a standard protocol.

Two University of Washington fourth-year students addressed this problem in the summer of 2025. Kaiis Jarrahy and Ben Schilpp, within the structure of UW's Program on the Environment Senior Capstone Research Project, travelled 966 miles across the Puget Lowlands in June and July, from the Cascades to the Olympic Mountains, from Bellingham to Randle. The examined sites which had been previously reported, roughly intersecting those reports with Washington State parks. This was not a systematic gridded search for the blight, nor a regional randomized search.

However, their strategy ensures that we now have reproducible reports from all areas within the Puget Lowlands. This definitively establishes that the blight is not simply a phenomenon of Seward Park's urban old-growth forest. Their work also creates a baseline for the sites useful for the future if tracking the phenomenon over time is pursued.
